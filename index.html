Two — three things gone wrong

Why it isn't working.

Modern farming feeds the world by doing one thing extraordinarily well: growing a single crop on a huge area. That trick has costs. Three of them matter for what comes next.

i.

The soil is dying

A handful of healthy soil contains more living things than there are people on Earth — fungi, bacteria, worms, mites. They make plants possible. Tilling, ploughing, and chemical sprays kill most of them. Dorset's topsoil is now six inches shallower than it was forty years ago. At current rates, much of England's farm soil has perhaps sixty harvests left in it.

ii.

The water carries the cost

Fertilisers and pesticides don't stay where they're sprayed. They run into streams — including Dorset's chalk streams, among the rarest freshwater habitats on Earth. Many are now silted, algae-choked, and nearly lifeless. The salmon and trout your grandparents fished are gone from places they thrived for a thousand years.

iii.

Your food got narrower

Three crops — wheat, rice, corn — provide nearly half of all human calories. Your supermarket has thousands of products built from the same dozen plants and the meat of a few breeds bred for industrial scale. Wild foods that fed people for thousands of years — hazelnuts, wild garlic, blackberries, mushrooms, watercress — are now luxuries or curiosities. Not because they don't grow well, but because no machine can pick them.

Four — the unlock

But how do we pick it?

This is the question that's stopped wild farming for a hundred years. A combine harvester needs a flat field of identical plants — one species, one height, one ripeness. A food forest is the opposite: every plant a different kind, ripening at a different time, hidden under leaves and in soil. Until now there was only one way to harvest it: by hand, slowly. So we never did, at scale.

What changes now: regenerative bots. Small machines built in the shape of the wild things whose work they share — a squirrel, a crow, a mole, a bee. They listen. They learn the land. They pick one fruit, one acorn, one handful at a time. They give way to anything wild that's already doing the job. They leave the soil richer than they found it. They are the tool that finally lets the land be what it wants to be.

Meet the first bot

Red — the squirrel bot

Gathering acorns under the oaks at Hooke Park, October

Red climbs anything an oak grows. She tests each acorn with a soft probe — too green, drop it; ripe and sound, twist it free, store it in her cheek pouch, carry it home. In a single autumn morning she can bring in twenty kilos of acorn mast from a single old oak — food for pigs, food for flour, and seed for the next generation of trees. Every tenth acorn she plants, not collects. The oaks she helps spread will outlive every person reading this. And she defers to every wild red squirrel she meets — pausing, stepping back, letting them feed first. The bots aren't here to replace the wild. They're here to help it return.

Red is the first. Here are some of the others — each one shaped after a wild creature whose work they share, each one designed to give way to the real thing wherever it still lives.

Bumble

Bee-sized. Pollinates flowers and harvests soft fruit one berry at a time. Defers to every wild bee it meets.

Crow

Magpie-sized. Gathers nuts and grain heads. Knows around 600 words. Brings small gifts home.

Mole

Burrows along fungal networks without breaking them. Lifts a single carrot when asked.

More food. More variety. Better food. Land that's getting richer instead of poorer. Animals living lives worth living. Streams full of fish again. And a small red squirrel called Red who'll bring you acorns in autumn, who plants every tenth one she finds, and who steps aside the moment a wild red squirrel comes near.

This isn't a fantasy of a smaller, simpler life. It's a fantasy of a more abundant one — made possible by regenerative bots that work in service of the wild, not in place of it. The land was always carrying more. We're just finally going to be able to count it — and pick it — and share it.

A vision in progress. Built with care, on chalk-grassland colours.
